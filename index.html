Website Redesign for Austin Non-profit

Save An Angel

In a 3 week sprint, my four person team redesigned the site for the non-profit organization Save An Angel with the ultimate goal of increasing community support for Save An Angel’s operation.

  • Date: 2019
  • Client: Save An Angel Non-profit Organization

My Role: UX/UI Designer

  • Design & Testing: survey, stakeholder meetings, in-person interviews, usability testing current site, AB testing lo-fi prototype
  • Analysis & Definition: affinity diagram, card-sorting, feature brainstorming and prioritization, user insight and problem statement
  • Design: wireframed homepage, low to high-fidelity homepage

The Final Design

Our goal for this redesign was to increase user support by simplifying and increasing the visbility of the paths of involvement, specifically the paths of donation, adoption, and volunteering. We aimed to provide crucial action. These are the flows on which we focused:

Stakeholder Interview

Our first step was to meet with our stakeholder: Jeremey Parks, the Executive Director of Save An Angel. From this meeting we were able to gain some valuable insights on Save An Angel's operation.

Business Model Canvas

With the insights gained from the stakeholder meeting, we drew up a business model canvas to analyze Save An Angel's system of operation.

SWOT Analysis

We conducted two SWOT Analyses, one for Save An Angel and another for Austin Pets Alive!, another successful pet rescue organization in Austin, TX. The two organizations have different niches in rescue, but both rely on community involvement.

Proto Personas

Save An Angel's website serves two important groups: the no-kill agencies who receive the rescues, and those in the Austin community who volunteer for or donate to Save An Angel. To keep those two groups in mind, we created two proto personas to guide our surveys and interviews.

Survey Insights

We made two surveys: one for receiving agencies, and one for potential donors and volunteers. The receiving agencies did not respond to our survey, but we receieved ample responses from potential volunteers and donors.

Usability Testing Current Site

We tested the current Save An Angel site, specifically looking at the routes of donation and volunteering. From our feedback, we formed some goals for our redesign.

Affinity Diagram & Empathy Map

Together we organized the data from our in-person interviews into an affinity diagram and empathy map. These exercises furthered our understanding of why users give to rescue organizations, what impedes giving, how users feel about giving and how they find out about opportunities.

Card Sorting

We card-sorted the navigation to make sure we had the most intuitive, efficient site layout.

Ideation & Feature Prioritization

Using the "I Like, I Wish, What If" method, we brainstormed potential features and ways the site could better serve its purposes. Then we ranked those features according to their priority and potential impact.

Storyboard

User Journey Maps

Usability Testing Feedback

  • Users wished that it said "Donate" instead of "Ways to Give" because that's the word for which their eye is scanning
  • Users wanted “Adoption” to be in the main menu to make it easier to find
  • Remove the facebook feed. Don’t direct users of the website to social media, because that draws them away from the site - where they would potentially donate
  • Improve the wording for the adoption process
  • Probably don’t need to be able to preview the adoption form, and it is taking away from the actual call to action

AB Testing Navigation Styles

We AB tested the 2 different navigation styles in both mobile and desktop.

Double Decker Horizontal Nav vs. Right-Side Vertical Nav

Vertical Swiping Nav vs. Hamburger Expanding Nav

AB Testing Feedback

  • User 1 felt more they were more familiar with the right side vertical navigation on desktop.
  • User 2 felt either desktop nav style could work, but to consider that the right side vertical menu decreases the screen space available for content.
  • Users were split on which mobile nav they liked better. They liked the smoothness and hierarchy of the hamburger expanding nav, but the personality and swiping action of the vertical swiping nav.

Final Nav

For our hi-fi desktop, we settled on a simple drop-down navigation because it is recognizable and comfortable for users, maintains the hierarchy, and conserves the most screen space compared to the other styles tested.

For our hi-fi mobile, we kept the playful swiping motion in the nav, but put the tab at the bottom of the screen to make it easy to reach with the thumb. We also integrated the drop-down hierarchy from the expanding hamburger version.

Takeaways

  • My team had to rely heavily on usability testing to make decisions, for example in deicidng on a navigation style. After receiving feedback, we decided to combine some aspects of the two styles for the most appealing and clear system.
  • Continuous communication of progress enabled us to move quickly as a team, to help each other with any tasks or roadblocks, and to quickly clear up any confusion.
  • We were not able to get survey responses from many receiving agencies. These agencies comprised one of our proto personas, and we had originally planned to research and improve upon the part of the site that serves their purposes. Because our data was limited, we focused instead on the volunteer/donor serving functions of the website.

Affinity Diagram & Empathy Map

Low-Fi Usability Testing Issues

After testing the lo-fi prototype I organized the feedback, paying special attention to the areas where most users struggled. These tests were a humbling and enlightening experience. I learned that people do not associate icons with the same things as me, and a misleading icon can throw off a user's whole interaction with the app. Furthermore, what seems so obvious to me, the designer, might only be obvious because I built it. Testing helped me look at my design with fresh eyes, to find issues to which I was blind.

High-Fi Usability Testing Issues

I implemented what I had learned from the low-fi usability tests in multiple iterations. Once I had a hi-fi prototype I did another round of tests, and was happy to see that the changes had improved usability. I used this round of feedback to tweak the hi-fi prototype.

The User Asks, We Deliver

Along with expanding the presence of the primary navigation, here are some ways the user feedback was implemented in the final prototype.

Takeaways

  1. Contrary to what I would like to believe, I do not intuitively know everything about the user. In order to make a product that has a place in the market and is enjoyable for users, I must rely on the data I receieve in surveys and interviews, and user test throughout the process.
  2. Repetition is not only unavoidable, but necessary in pushing and developing the design.
  3. Do not be intimidated by new challenges, and don't be afraid to ask for help or guidance. There is so much to learn as a budding designer, but people are kind and there are awesome resources everywhere.

Ecommerce Flow for Brand-New Grocery Store Concept

H-E-B e-z

In a 5 week sprint, my 5 person team and I conceptualized, designed, prototyped, tested, and coded a responsive e-commerce website called H-E-B e-z, which is designed to service a brick-and-mortar convenience grocery store.

  • Date: November - December 2019

My Role

UX/UI Designer

  • Collaborated on concept ideation
  • Conducted in-person user interviews
  • Created affinity diagram, empathy map, and business model canvas
  • Created sketches and wireframes
  • AB / Usability tested low-fi prototype
  • Created high-fi homepage and prototype of Search and Add to Cart flow

Developer

  • Coded the Search and Add to Cart flow, consisting of the search results page and three product detail pages

Ethnographic Observations

See sources
  1. There has been a dramatic increase in online grocery shopping in recent years driven largely by millenials (5)
  2. Online grocery shoppers are more likely to be men ages 18-44 and/or Amazon Prime members (1)
  3. Saving time & convenience are key components: (6, 7, 8)
    • Prepared meal solutions and meal kits have become very popular due to "time starvation" (6, 8)
    • A smaller store = faster shopping experience (8)
  4. Millennials shop for groceries differently than previous generations: They are not as brand-loyal. Instead of one large trip per week, millenials tend to make quick trips to multiple stores and buy a few items from each (3, 4, 6, 8)
  5. Millenials have an interest in healthy eating, and though price conscious, are willing to invest in local or ethically-made products (7)
  6. Grocery shoppers now have an expectation of speed and convenience (7, 8)
