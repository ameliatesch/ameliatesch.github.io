Save An Angel

Website Redesign

My team and I redesigned the site for the non-profit organization Save An Angel to efficiently inform and direct potential volunteers and donors to ways they can help the animal rescue mission, with the overall goal of increasing overall support for Save An Angel’s operation.

  • Date: 2019
  • Client: Save An Angel Non-profit Organization
  • My Role: UX Designer

Stakeholder Meeting

Our first step was to meet with our stakeholder: Jeremey Parks, the Executive Director of Save An Angel. From this meeting we gained some valuable insights on Save An Angel's operation:

  • Save An Angel consists of 3 primary programs: Rescue Activities, Community Outreach and Operation Relocation
  • People find out about Save An Angel mostly through social media or word-of-mouth.
  • Most people access the website through their mobile device
  • Most all of their funding comes from individual contributions, and they rely on many volunteers to operate
  • There are 2 main groups who use the site: receiving agencies and individuals who want to help the cause

Problem Statement

Save An Angel is a pet rescue organization that requires the help of community giving and involvement to fuel its mission efforts. Through our 7 user interviews and 73 survey results, we discovered that there is a high interest in getting involved in different areas of the animal rescue mission, including volunteering, adopting, fostering, donating and supporting fundraising events, but that users are less likely to help as the effort or resources required increases. Therefore, users need a way to give back with minimal user effort and roadblocks.

The avenues for involvement are not all presented clearly and consistently on the Save An Angel website. How might we redesign the site to maximize visibility and simplicity of the paths to involvement so that users can quickly gather information and complete their goals with minimal effort and expense, ultimately increasing user involvement?

Usability Testing and AB Testing Navigation Styles

We created and tested 2 different navigation styles in both mobile and desktop. One had the secondary navigation as a horizontal layer underneath the primary navigation, and the other had the secondary navigation as a right-side vertical menu. Our feedback:

  • Users wished that it said “Donate” instead of “Ways to Give”
  • Users wanted “Adoption” to be in the main menu to make it easier to find
  • User 1 felt more they were more used to the right side vertical navigation on desktop. User 2 felt either could work, but to consider that the right side vertical menu decreases space on screen for content.
  • Remove the facebook feed. Don’t direct users of the website to social media, because that draws them away from the site - where they would potentially donate.
  • Improve the wording for the adoption process.
  • Probably don’t need to be able to preview the adoption form, and it is taking away from the actual call to action
  • Users were split on which mobile nav they liked better. They liked the smoothness and hierarchy of Emma’s mobile nav, but the personality and swiping action of Augie’s mobile nav. We could potentially combine them.

Takeaways

  • My team had to rely heavily on usability testing to make decisions, for example in deicidng on a navigation style. After receiving feedback, we decided to combine some aspects of the two styles for the most appealing and clear system.
  • Continuous communication of progress enabled us to move quickly as a team, to help each other with any tasks or roadblocks, and to quickly clear up any confusion.
  • We were not able to get survey responses from many receiving agencies. These agencies comprised one of our proto personas, and we had originally planned to research and improve upon the part of the site that serves their purposes. Because our data was limited, we focused instead on the volunteer/donor serving functions of the website, for which we were able to gather data through surveys and interviews.

TripJar

Mobile App Design

TripJar is a mobile app that simplifies and quickens group trip planning using the fun, interactive behaviors of social media. Through the app users can search for trip activities, restaurants, and places to stay, and send those ideas directly to their trip companions for a vote.

  • Date: 2019
  • My Role: UX & UI Designer

User Research

My first crucial step towards creating an app that would resonate with users was finding out all about their experiences with travel planning. I conducted a survey and 5 in-person interviews with subjects that varied in age, income, and cultural group.

Survey Insights

  • 31% of participants reported feeling overwhelmed with the travel planning process, while 34.5% felt the process made them feel anxious. 31% reported feeling stressed by the process and 17.5% reported feeling impatient
  • 69% of participants reported that they consider family or other parties when planning their travel.
  • 82.8% of participants suggested that the process of sharing of travel information could be improved upon.
  • In a free response asking how they coordinate with their travel parties, 43.5% of participants reported multiple modes of communication.

Interview Insights

  • 100% of subjects report coordinating with friends and family to make plans
  • Multiple users reported that they really enjoy the planning process
  • Most all subjects prefer to have most of the trip planned, but either reserve flexible chunks of time or are comfortable with deviation
  • All interviewees had certain preferences for how they liked to travel, but not all were heavily involved in planning. Those who didn’t plan as much usually traveled with a more avid planner.
  • Although all those interviewed reported they enjoyed travel, many found the travel planning process stressful or intimidating because it was a large undertaking

Affinity Diagram & Empathy Map

User Persona

Problem Statement

The method, extent and opinion of trip planning varies greatly from person to person. Planning a trip with a group can easily become complex because the group must cater to the different schedules, personalities and preferences of all its members. Users report either having to coordinate in person meetings or using multiple apps to communicate and plan. Furthermore, although some users want to be more involved than others, most travelers still want a say in their itinerary. There are myriad travel apps that aid in finding ideas and resources, packing, and scheduling the trip but are not designed for multiple planners. How might we create a space that encourages all trip companions to be involved in the planning processto whatever extent each member chooses, in order to maximize trip planning efficiency?

Feature Prioritization

Main Focuses

  • A profile for each trip that would store that trip’s information and be shared with all members of the trip party
  • Sending ideas to a trip profile
  • Voting on trip ideas by the members
